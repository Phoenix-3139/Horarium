Title: "Berlin in Motion / Bewegtes Berlin" Description: This course analyzes long twentieth-century German culture through the lens of Berlin’s status as a metropolis in constant flux. Place of rapid industrialization, fertile ground for cultural experimentation in the Weimar Republic, site of rubble after the Third Reich’s fall, divided city during the Cold War, capital of reunified Germany, magnet for artists and hub for cultural diversity today: Berlin’s energy derives from its continuous readjustments. We will study a variety of texts (poetry, narrative prose, journalistic writing) and visual media (painting, photography, film) to discuss Berlin’s culture and trace its turbulent history from the late nineteenth century to the present. The language of instruction, reading, and discussion in this course is German. Emphasis will be placed on honing listening and reading skills, refining oral and written expression, with a select review of grammar. Taught IN GERMAN.

Title: "Autobiographical Crash Sites: The Drama of Internal Alterity and Political Compromise" Description: We shall examine a wide range of texts that confront a history of self in a compromised world where the very premises of selfhood must be constantly discarded and reinvented, part of a shattering linked to difficult growth spurts, politically stunted. Readings span from Rousseau and Goethe to James Baldwin, Walter Benjamin, Freud, Hannah Arendt, Kafka, Chantal Akerman, Werner Herzog and Sandy Stone, whose pathbreaking Post-Transsexual Manifesto provides some of the theoretical groundwork for establishing a hermeneutics of Bildung and transition, a recast of autobiographical theories and crucial psychoanalytic checkpoints. The failure of self-appropriation is a theme driven by Nietzsche in a number of morphs dependent on the often repressed inclusion of “the woman in Nietzsche.” Participants will be encouraged to work on their own projects that use this course as a jumping-off point for theoretical engagements with the frustrated development of self and history in an era of incessant traumatization. Taught in English.

Title: "Contemporary German Theater & Performance – Deconstructing, Decolonizing, and De-Choreographing the Canon" Description: For US audiences, German theater often has the reputation of being bizarre, loud, confusing, other-worldly, excessive, excessively long, filled with screaming, naked bodies covered in fluids – and in German. German theater also has been nominated as a world heritage site by UNESCO, as one of the most formally, politically, and culturally diverse art panoramas on planet Earth. And one of the most generously funded to boot: the city of Berlin alone has an annual cultural budget more than four times larger than the entire US National Endowment for the Arts. In this course, we will examine key artists, ensembles, institutions, and theoretical frameworks for making sense of, learning to love, and also critically questioning the German theater. Artists and groups under consideration include Thomas Ostermeier, Milo Rau, Yael Ronen, Marie Schleef, Vinge & Müller, Rimini Protokoll, Gob Squad, She She Pop, andcompany&Co, and others. Alongside new playwriting, performance art, and dance, we will explore pressing topics that include postmigrant theater, feminist theater, documentary theater, "directors' theater," and postdramatic theater. Students will respond to the course material through their own creative interventions and explorations. For those of us steeped in Anglo-American naturalism, this course will offer a radically different perspective on what is possible at the theater. (Taught in English. No knowledge of the German language is required.) Course originates as DRLIT-UA609.001

GERM-UA 9003 Intermediate German I

Intermediate German I is the first part of a two-semester-long intermediate sequence. You will continue to study grammar, vocabulary and other aspects of language.
