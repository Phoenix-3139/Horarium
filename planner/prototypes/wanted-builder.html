Wanted-builder prototypes

Five takes on the same expression: ENGR-UH 1000 AND (ENGR-UH 2010 OR ENGR-UH 2011) AND CORE-UH 1234 AND (CHEM-UH 1 OR CHEM-UH 2). All variants share the same data shape, same drag/click affordances, and assume the picker's + Want pill is the primary entry point. Pick the one you want and we'll wire it.

A · Formula Math-expression one-liner

Reads left-to-right like an equation. Chips are courses, italic ∧/∨ are operators, parentheses bracket a sub-group. Click any operator to flip it; click a paren to expand the group inline.

ENGR-UH 1000 ∧ ( ENGR-UH 2010 ∨ ENGR-UH 2011 ) ∧ CORE-UH 1234 ∧ ( CHEM-UH 1 ∨ CHEM-UH 2 )
Strengths: compact; the formula is unambiguous; operator symbols carry meaning at a glance.
Risks: ∧/∨ glyphs may be unfamiliar to non-CS students; long expressions wrap and lose the linear feel; paren drop-targets are small.
B · Columns Kanban: one column per requirement

Each column = one thing the schedule must satisfy (an AND clause). Stacked cards inside a column = alternatives (OR). To pick one, drop a course in. Wide layout naturally fills horizontal space below the calendar.

Strengths: intuitive (kanban metaphor); horizontal by design; deep nesting is impossible — that's a feature, not a bug, since 99% of real degree planning is just AND-of-OR.
Risks: can't express OR-of-ANDs without flattening; a hard ceiling on logic complexity (we'd document this explicitly: "for richer expressions, switch to formula mode").
C · Sentence Plain-English with inline pills

Reads as a sentence. The expression is rendered into prose with operator words in italics and OR-groups bracketed by a tinted box. Friendliest for first-timers; least visual for power users.

I want ENGR-UH 1000 and ENGR-UH 2010 or ENGR-UH 2011 and CORE-UH 1234 and CHEM-UH 1 or CHEM-UH 2 …
Strengths: zero learning curve; reads like what it means.
Risks: long expressions wrap awkwardly; less structurally clear when you have many parallel ORs; "and" / "or" words look like prose, not affordances — discoverability cost.

Strengths: structure is unambiguous; works at any nesting depth; familiar metaphor (file tree, json viewer).
Risks: vertical-heavy → eats the horizontal space; rails feel CS-y; lots of visual lines.
E · Block Pretty-printed code-block formula

Like a programming editor. Indented, monospaced, with the operators as italic keywords. Useful if the user thinks in code.

# Schedule must satisfy: and [ ENGR-UH 1000, or [ENGR-UH 2010, ENGR-UH 2011], CORE-UH 1234, or [CHEM-UH 1, CHEM-UH 2], ]
Strengths: exact and unambiguous; nesting is instantly readable for engineers; copyable as a "saved query".
Risks: intimidates non-coders; doesn't feel like part of the rest of the app; brackets and commas are syntax noise.

My pick: B (Kanban columns) feels like the best match for the actual problem — most NYUAD scheduling cases really are AND-of-OR ("I need 1000, one of these labs, the writing seminar, and one of these electives") and column metaphor maps directly onto that. Variant A is the runner-up if you want to allow the occasional weird nested case.
